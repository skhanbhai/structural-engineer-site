Section / 01 Quick answers

Quick answers about wall cracks.

If you are standing in front of a crack at 11pm with a torch in one hand and a mug of tea in the other, here is the short version. The longer version is further down.

Are cracks serious?

Most cracks in plaster and render are not. Hairline cracks around door frames, along ceiling and wall junctions or on fresh plaster are usually cosmetic. Cracks worth a closer look tend to be wider, run at an angle, reappear after filling or show up on both sides of a wall.

When should you worry?

It is the pattern that matters more than any single crack. Wider than about 3 to 5 mm, diagonal across a wall, around openings or visibly bigger over a few weeks are all signals worth acting on. Doors and windows suddenly sticking is another sign a wall or floor has moved.

Do I need a structural engineer?

Not always. A chartered structural engineer is useful when you need a written judgement for a solicitor, insurer or buyer, or when you need help deciding whether to repair or investigate further. Send photos first and we will give you an honest steer before suggesting a paid site visit.

Section / 02 Warning signs

Warning signs worth checking.

Any one of these on its own is rarely alarming. Several together, or one getting worse over time, is when a second opinion from a chartered structural engineer is genuinely useful.

!

Widening cracks

Cracks that are visibly larger than they were a few weeks or months ago. Especially if filling has already been tried and failed.

!

Diagonal cracks

Cracks running at an angle across walls, particularly in masonry or brickwork. Often a sign of differential movement of the foundation below.

!

Recurring cracks

Cracks that come back in the same place after being filled and redecorated. The underlying movement has not stopped.

!

Doors or windows sticking

Frames suddenly binding, latches not aligning or gaps opening at the head or sill. These often indicate that a wall or floor has shifted.

!

Cracks around openings

Stepped cracks radiating from the corners of windows and doors are common where a lintel is overloaded or the wall is moving around it.

!

New cracks after building work

New or reopened cracks that appeared after an extension, a neighbour’s build, a loft conversion or nearby excavation work.

If more than one of these sounds familiar, send a few photos for an initial view by a chartered structural engineer.

Section / 03 Common causes

Common causes of cracks.

Most residential cracking comes down to one of four causes. The job of an inspection is to work out which, and to rule out the ones that matter.

01

Settlement

New extensions and new builds bed down over the first couple of years. Fine cracks that appear and stabilise are usually settlement, not structural failure.

02

Thermal movement

Walls and ceilings expand and contract with temperature. Hairline cracks that open and close with the seasons, especially near windows or roofs, are normal.

03

Shrinkage

Fresh plaster, new render and drying timber all shrink as they cure. Vertical cracks on new plaster or at corners usually fall into this bucket.

04

Structural movement

Foundation movement, overloaded lintels, subsidence, heave or issues from nearby building works. This is the category that warrants a written engineering view.

Section / 07 FAQ

Crack inspection FAQs.

If yours is not here, send it via the form. We read every enquiry.

Are cracks in walls serious?

Most are not. Cracks in paint, plaster or along fresh seams are usually cosmetic and come from shrinkage, thermal movement or minor settlement. They become more serious when wider than about 3 to 5 mm, diagonal, recurring after filling or visibly growing over weeks. The pattern across a wall matters more than any single crack.

When should I call a structural engineer for cracks?

Useful triggers are doors or windows suddenly sticking, cracks visible from both sides of a wall, cracks that reappear after filling or new cracks that appeared after building work nearby. Also call if a buyer, seller, solicitor, insurer or mortgage lender has asked for a written structural view. If you are not sure, send photos and we will tell you honestly whether a paid inspection is worth it.

Can I send photos before booking a site visit?

Please do. A few well-lit photos showing the whole wall and a close-up of the crack, ideally with something for scale like a coin or ruler, is usually enough for an initial view. Send them on WhatsApp along with your postcode and a short note.

How much does a crack inspection cost?

Reviewing photos and giving an initial view is free. A site inspection for a typical London residential property is charged as a fixed fee, confirmed in writing once we have seen photos and confirmed access. A signed written report for solicitors, insurers, mortgage lenders or buyers is an additional fixed fee. The full amount is quoted before any work starts.

Do you inspect cracks before buying a property?

Yes. Pre-purchase crack inspections are a common request, often triggered by a homebuyer survey flagging cracking or movement that needs a chartered structural engineer’s view. We can review the survey extract and photos first, then carry out a site visit and signed report suitable for your solicitor or mortgage lender if it is needed.

Can you provide reports for solicitors, insurers or mortgage lenders?

Yes. Reports are signed and stamped by a chartered engineer of the Institution of Structural Engineers and written in a format accepted by solicitors, insurers and mortgage lenders. Timescales are confirmed in writing before booking.

Do you cover Putney and South West London?

Yes. Putney, Wandsworth, Fulham and the wider South West London area are part of our regular residential coverage. Send your postcode with photos and we will confirm timings for a site visit if one is needed.

Do you cover Harrow and North West London?

Yes. Harrow, Barnet, Edgware, Hendon and the wider North West London area are within our regular coverage. The fastest start is a few photos and your postcode. We will reply with an initial view and confirm next steps.

Is this subsidence?

Possibly, but probably not. Subsidence is specific: the ground beneath the foundation has moved, usually because of clay shrinkage, nearby trees or drainage leaks. It typically shows as diagonal cracks wider at the top than the bottom, affects both sides of a wall and appears alongside doors or windows that stop closing. Most cracks we see are not subsidence, but if the pattern fits we will say so clearly and advise on the right next step, often a conversation with your buildings insurer.

Will repairs need Building Control approval?

Sometimes. Cosmetic redecoration does not, but structural repairs such as new lintels, tie bars or underpinning usually do. Where Building Control approval is needed, we prepare the calculations and drawings against the relevant GOV.UK building regulations and, if helpful, submit them through the Planning Portal building control service.
