Section / 01 Quick answers

The three questions we hear every week.

If you’re here at 11pm with a torch in one hand and a mug of tea in the other: here’s the short version. The longer version is further down.

Are cracks serious?

Most cracks in plaster and render are not. Hairline cracks around door frames, along ceiling–wall junctions, or on fresh plaster are usually cosmetic - shrinkage, thermal movement, or settlement of a recent finish. Serious cracks tend to be wider, run at an angle, reappear after filling, or show up on both sides of a wall.

When should you worry?

Think of it as a pattern, not a single data point. Wider than about 3–5 mm, diagonal across a wall, around openings, or getting visibly bigger over a few weeks - those are signals worth acting on. Doors and windows suddenly sticking is another sign a wall or floor has moved.

Do I need a structural engineer?

Not always. A structural engineer becomes useful when you need a written judgement - e.g. for a solicitor, insurer, buyer, or to decide whether to repair or investigate further. We’ll usually give an honest steer from the photos before suggesting a paid site visit.

Section / 02 Warning signs

Cracks that are worth a closer look.

Any one of these in isolation is usually not alarming. Several together, or one getting worse, is when a structural engineer is a useful second opinion.

!

Widening cracks

Cracks that are visibly larger than they were a few weeks or months ago - especially if filling has already been tried and failed.

!

Diagonal cracks

Cracks running at an angle across walls, particularly in masonry or brickwork. Often a sign of differential movement of the foundation below.

!

Recurring cracks

Cracks that come back in the same place after being filled and redecorated. The underlying movement hasn’t stopped.

!

Doors or windows sticking

Frames suddenly binding, latches not aligning, or gaps opening at the head or sill - indicators that a wall or floor has shifted.

!

Cracks around openings

Stepped cracks radiating from the corners of windows and doors are common where a lintel is overloaded or the wall is moving around it.

!

New cracks after building work

New or reopened cracks that appeared after an extension, a neighbour’s build, a loft conversion, or nearby excavation work.

Section / 03 Common causes

Where cracks usually come from.

Most residential cracking is one of four things. The job of an inspection is to work out which - and to rule out the ones that matter.

01

Settlement

New extensions and new builds bed down over the first couple of years. Fine cracks that appear and stabilise are usually settlement, not structural failure.

02

Thermal movement

Walls and ceilings expand and contract with temperature. Hairline cracks that open and close with the seasons - especially near windows or roofs - are normal.

03

Shrinkage

Fresh plaster, new render, and drying timber all shrink as they cure. Vertical cracks on new plaster or at corners usually fall in this bucket.

04

Structural movement

Foundation movement, overloaded lintels, subsidence, heave, or issues from nearby building works. This is the category that warrants a written engineering view.

Section / 06 FAQ

Questions homeowners ask us.

If yours isn’t here, send it via the form - we read every enquiry.

Are cracks in walls serious?

Most aren’t. Cracks in paint, plaster, or along fresh seams are usually cosmetic - caused by shrinkage, thermal movement, or minor settlement. Cracks become more serious when they’re wider than 3–5 mm, diagonal, recurring after filling, around door or window openings, or visibly growing over weeks.

The pattern matters more than any one crack. A single hairline crack is almost always fine; several stepped diagonal cracks following a line of mortar are worth an engineer’s view.

When should I call a structural engineer for cracks?

Useful triggers: doors or windows that suddenly stick, cracks you can see from both sides of a wall, cracks that reappear after filling, or new cracks that appeared after building work nearby. Also: if a buyer, seller, solicitor, or insurer has asked for a written structural view, that’s a clear case for an engineer’s report.

If you’re not sure, send photos - we’ll tell you honestly whether a paid inspection is worth it.

Can I ignore small cracks?

Small, stable, hairline cracks - yes, in most cases. Fill them when you next decorate and keep an eye on them. If one comes back, widens, or joins up with others, that’s the signal to get advice. Taking a photo next to a ruler every few weeks is a simple way to track whether a crack is stable or changing.

How much does a crack inspection cost?

A site inspection for a typical London residential property is a fixed fee, quoted once we’ve seen photos and confirmed access - usually in the range of a few hundred pounds. A signed written report (for solicitors, insurers or buyers) is an additional fixed fee on top.

We quote the full amount in writing before any work starts. No surprises.

Do you cover all of London?

Yes - across Greater London and the commuter belt in the South East. Most of our residential work is in the boroughs of Fulham, Hammersmith, Wandsworth, Lambeth, Southwark, Camden, Islington, Hackney, Haringey and out to Brent, Ealing, Richmond and Kingston, but we travel to any postcode within reach of a day visit.

Can I send photos before booking a site visit?

Please do - it’s the fastest way to a useful answer. Two or three well-lit photos showing the whole wall and a close-up of the crack (with something for scale, like a coin or ruler) is usually enough for a first view. You can attach them to the enquiry form below or paste links to a shared folder.

Is this subsidence?

Possibly, but probably not. Subsidence is specific: the ground beneath the foundation has moved, usually because of clay shrinkage, nearby trees, or drainage leaks. It usually shows as diagonal cracks wider at the top than the bottom, affecting both sides of a wall, and appearing alongside doors or windows that stop closing. Most cracks we see are not subsidence - but if the pattern fits, we’ll say so clearly and advise on the right next step (often a chat with your buildings insurer).

Do you provide reports for solicitors, insurers, and mortgage lenders?

Yes. Reports are signed and stamped by a chartered structural engineer (IStructE) and written in a format solicitors, insurers and mortgage lenders accept. Turnaround is typically five working days from the site visit.
